Statement of Purpose  
Master of Applied Computer Science

Computer vision underpins my engineering progression toward building systems resilient to uncertainties of the physical world. My first encounter with visual computing was through a black box—pre-built computer vision capabilities from cloud platforms that developers can integrate into applications without prior knowledge. I began testing an OCR API with photos of grocery receipts I had been taking for budget tracking, and this experience sparked my curiosity. The techniques that accurately extracted item names and prices from my crumpled receipts could also guide robots in warehouses and homes, but the algorithms working behind the scenes remained opaque.

My first software engineering role brought me back to computer vision in a new context—its function within purely rule-bound multi-sensor systems. I developed core features for the fleet management system that controls over sixty automated guided vehicles at South Korea's largest and first fully automated container terminal. My primary focus was collision management and vehicle allocation. 

Within days of joining, there was an incident where a vehicle struck a cornerstone at the edge of the operational map performing an alignment movement. Preventing similar collisions became my first task. I defined high-risk zones and implemented alternative routing based on the clearance requirements of curving and reversing maneuvers, mitigating collision risks  effectively. However, it was a reactive fix tailored to an edge case, one that works but does not scale.

These large-scale industrial robots relied on a limited sensor suite—2D LiDAR and physical contact sensors—and software tightly coupled with pre-mapped routes. While vehicles could detect line-of-sight obstacles and execute emergency stops, they lacked the ability to track objects and anticipate hazards in dynamic surroundings. This rigidity ruled out autonomous recovery and path replanning, causing recurring traffic bottlenecks across the terminal and frequent intervention from human operators.

I found the work deeply rewarding, as each improvement delivered tangible results such as reduced downtime and increased throughput. At the same time, the experience revealed how much manual engineering effort was needed to compensate for systems without environmental adaptability, a limitation shared across domains. Recognizing this shortcoming firsthand deepened my interest in how to translate raw sensory input into spatial reasoning that enables genuine autonomy.

The perception techniques that resolve industrial rigidity apply equally to more personal contexts, such as robotic mobility aids with scene understanding or assistive environments with fall detection. These everyday systems demand the same technical sophistication as large-scale automation, yet have direct individual impact. My goal is to build autonomous systems that continuously adjust to visual feedback in unstructured environments across logistics, robotics, and assistive technology.

The Master of Applied Computer Science offers theoretical rigor and applied training in computer vision, machine learning, and dependable programming principles necessary to transform these challenges into deployable solutions. COMP 6341 Computer Vision and COMP 6711 Computational Geometry address the limited spatiotemporal awareness I encountered through geometric vision and motion planning algorithms, and COMP 6801 Deep Learning cover sequence models and advanced neural architectures fundamental to adaptive planning and decision-making.

Hands-on experience has built my engineering confidence. I learned most by tackling concrete industrial problems with competing constraints between safety and efficiency. The applied methodology of the program combining project-focused coursework aligns closely with my learning path. I am ready to contribute meaningfully during co-op placements and beyond in Montreal's technology ecosystem, delivering vision-driven autonomous systems that respond to real-world needs.
